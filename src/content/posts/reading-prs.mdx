A friend of mine teaches a graduate-level fiction workshop. She told me once that the way she figures out a new class is to read the first short story each student turns in. Not the polished one — the *first* one. The hasty, slightly anxious one. The one written before they know what the room sounds like.

You can tell, she said, what someone is going to write all year from that first story.

I think pull requests are the same.

## What a PR is, actually

A PR is the smallest possible artifact that contains:

- A constraint the author accepted (the title)
- The author's idea of how to solve it (the diff)
- The author's idea of how to talk to their colleagues about it (the description)
- The team's idea of how to talk back (the review)
- A trace of what changed under pressure (the force-pushes)

That's a lot. That's basically the team, compressed.

If you want to understand a codebase, sure, read the code. If you want to understand a *team*, read the PRs.

## How to read one

I read PRs the way I read short stories: cold, no context, in order.

1. **The title.** Is it a sentence, or a fragment? Does it say what or why?
2. **The description.** Is there one? Does it contain "why now"? Does it link to anything?
3. **The diff.** Is it focused, or did three things land at once?
4. **The first review comment.** Is it nitpicky, generous, evasive? Whose voice is loudest in the team?
5. **The resolution.** How long was the back-and-forth? Who blinked?

You can tell, from those five things, more than I've gotten from any onboarding doc.

## The Friday-afternoon PR

The PR I want to read most is the one merged at 4:53pm on a Friday.

That PR is honest. The author is tired. They want to be done. The reviewer either lets it slide or doesn't. And the team's actual standards — not the ones written in the wiki — are revealed in that one decision.

I have, more than once, made a decision about whether to join a team based on a single Friday PR.

## A defense of long descriptions

The most underrated skill on a team is writing a PR description that someone reads voluntarily.

Most teams don't ask for these. Some teams forbid them ("the diff should speak for itself"). I think both groups are wrong.

A good PR description is not the same as a good commit message. A commit message answers "what changed". A PR description answers "why this, why now, why this way, and what did you almost do instead."

That last part — *what you almost did instead* — is the one most people leave out. It's also the most useful sentence in the entire PR. It tells the reviewer where the load-bearing decision was, which means they can review it.

## A short reading list

If you want to practice, here's the equivalent of a graduate seminar reading list:

- Linux kernel: read three reverts in a row. Watch what gets reverted, by whom, with what tone.
- Rails: read any PR that involves David Heinemeier Hansson. Whether you agree or not, he has a *voice*.
- Postgres: read the discussion thread, not the commit. Postgres development happens in the mailing list.
- Any company you'd want to work at, if their main repo is public.

You will, after about ten PRs, know whether you'd be happy there. Faster than any blog post. Faster than any recruiter pitch.

The PRs are the team. Read them.
