I have a soft spot for teams that A/B test everything. They're at least *trying* to ask the question "does this work?"

But I want to talk about a specific failure mode I've watched four times now: A/B testing the microcopy on a button.

## The trap

It usually goes like this. A designer writes "Continue." A PM writes "Get started." A growth person writes "Start your free trial." Nobody wants to fight, so they run an experiment.

Two weeks later: "Start your free trial" wins by 2.4%, p = 0.07. Ship it.

A month later: someone proposes "Begin free trial." Run it again.

This is a treadmill. The variance in a button label is usually smaller than the variance in your daily traffic. You are watching noise, and the noise is telling you to ship whichever variant happened to have a slightly better Tuesday.

## What I do instead

Three drafts. Ship the second.

The first draft is what you'd say to your most cynical friend. The second is that, but rewritten so a stranger can understand it. The third is what marketing would write if marketing were in the room.

You ship the second.

The first is too cool. The third is too eager. The second is the one that makes the user feel respected.

If you can't decide between drafts: read each one aloud. Whichever one you said with the most natural cadence wins. This works ~90% of the time.

## When testing actually pays

I'm not anti-experiment. Test:

- **The flow** — should the trial signup come before or after the demo?
- **The price** — does $9/mo land differently than $12/mo?
- **The headline** — what is this product, in one sentence, that people respond to?

Those are real decisions. The button text on step 3 of the signup is not a real decision. It is a decision you can make in 4 minutes by reading the page out loud.

## The cost of testing trivial things

There's a hidden cost to A/B testing everything: it teaches your team that they don't have to write well, because the framework will sort it out for them.

It will not. The framework just tells you which of two mediocre options is mediocre by 2%.

Write the copy yourself. Read it aloud. Ship the second draft. Move on to a problem that actually matters.
